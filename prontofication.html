Exhibit 01

The Craigslist Moment for
Indian Home Services

Just as Craigslist was unbundled into Airbnb (stays), Indeed (jobs), Tinder (dating), and Etsy (crafts) — Urban Company's horizontal home services platform is being decomposed into vertical-specific, speed-first startups that own a single use case deeply.

The Pattern

Every horizontal marketplace eventually gets unbundled by vertical specialists who offer 10× better experience in a single category. Urban Company is India's Craigslist of home services — and the unbundling has begun.

The Catalyst

Quick commerce trained India's urban consumers to expect 10-minute delivery. That same expectation now applies to home services — sweeping, cooking, laundry, beauty — all in under 15 minutes.

Exhibit 06

Six Principles of Pronto-fication

Investment principles and design thinking distilled from the emergent pattern of home services unbundling. Each principle reveals what makes this cycle different.

01
Principle 01

Win Micromarkets, Not Cities

In hyperlocal services, you win or lose at the 1.5-2km radius level. Snabbit's median walking distance between jobs: 250m. The game is density, not breadth.

Strategic Lens

This mirrors how Zepto won quick commerce — by building dark store networks in high-density clusters before going wide. Geographic concentration creates defensibility.

02
Principle 02

Frequency Creates Moats

Daily cleaning has fundamentally different economics than monthly beauty services. High-frequency categories build habit loops, reduce CAC through repeat usage, and create referral compounding.

Strategic Lens

The unbundlers target ₹150/hr tasks because frequency trumps ticket size. A customer who books 20 times/month at ₹200 is worth more than one who books once at ₹2,000.

03
Principle 03

Supply is the Moat, Not Demand

99.99% of this market is offline. Demand exists. The constraint is structured, reliable, trained supply deployed within walking distance. Pronto's referral-led supply growth compounds at 20% WoW.

Strategic Lens

This is an operational moat, not a technical one. Whoever trains, retains, and positions workers best wins. Supply-side network effects in micromarkets create local monopolies.

04
Principle 04

The Incumbent's Dilemma

Urban Company's InstaHelp is growing fast (51K daily bookings) but is dilutive to the core business: ₹21Cr quarterly loss, ₹381 loss per order. The startup attackers have no legacy margins to protect.

Strategic Lens

Classic innovator's dilemma: UC must invest in InstaHelp to defend against unbundlers, but the economics threaten its newly-achieved profitability narrative as a public company.

05
Principle 05

Variable Cost > Fixed Cost = Advantage

Unlike quick commerce with its dark stores (capex-heavy), instant home services run on variable cost structures. Workers are the infrastructure. No warehouses, no cold chains.

Strategic Lens

Pronto's founder noted: "The cost structure is more in our favour" vs. quick commerce. Total aggregate burn should be lower because there's less capex. This is a margin advantage at scale.

06
Principle 06

Trust Inside the Home

This isn't delivering a grocery bag. A worker enters your home. Background verification, training, insurance, SOS features — these aren't nice-to-haves, they're table stakes.

Strategic Lens

The trust barrier is what kept this market 99% offline. Whoever builds the highest-trust brand in each city wins disproportionately. Trust doesn't unbundle — it concentrates.

Exhibit 07

Risk-Reward Matrix

Every structural shift carries embedded risks. Understanding both sides of each risk is essential for clear-eyed assessment of the Pronto-fication thesis.

High

Burn Rate Escalation

The Risk

InstaHelp lost ₹61Cr adjusted EBITDA in one quarter. Startups face similar burn dynamics with aggressive pricing.

The Counter

Variable cost structure means burn can be modulated. No fixed dark store costs.

Medium

Winner-Take-Most Isn't Guaranteed

The Risk

Unlike ride-hailing where one app dominates, home services may support 2-3 players per city due to supply constraints.

The Counter

Micromarket density creates local monopolies. Multiple winners across different micromarkets.

High

Supply Attrition

The Risk

Workers can leave for competitors or return to informal arrangements. Retention is the hardest problem in gig platforms.

The Counter

Better pay (₹25-30K vs ₹9K informal), insurance, structured shifts. Worker NPS is high at Pronto/Snabbit.

Medium

Regulatory Risk

The Risk

UC's 'Insta Maids' naming controversy shows sensitivity around domestic work. Labour unions may push for employee classification.

The Counter

Platforms are formally better for workers than informal alternatives. Proactive worker benefits help narrative.

Medium

Consumer Price Sensitivity

The Risk

Can you raise AOV from ₹149 to ₹300+ without destroying demand? Premium vs. volume tension.

The Counter

Convenience premium exists. Post-COVID trust concerns drive willingness to pay. Habit formation reduces price sensitivity.

Low

Horizontal Re-bundling

The Risk

Urban Company or a super-app (Zepto, Swiggy) could re-bundle services, eliminating vertical advantage.

The Counter

Home services require deep operational excellence per category. Re-bundling doesn't solve the quality problem.

Bottom Line

India's $60B home services market is <1% online. The unbundling of Urban Company is not a competitive response — it's a structural inevitability. The question isn't whether it happens, but who wins each micromarket.
